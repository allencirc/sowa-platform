# Content-Type Guides

This document provides detailed guidance for creating and editing each of the five content types on the SOWA platform. Use this alongside the admin guide for reference on required and optional fields, domain-specific tips, and best practices specific to offshore wind energy.

---

## Careers

A career listing represents a job role within the offshore wind energy sector. Career content helps platform users understand what roles exist, what they entail, and how to progress into or between them.

### Core Fields

**Slug** (Required)
The URL identifier for this career (e.g. `wind-turbine-technician`). It is auto-generated from the title but can be customised before creation. Once the career is created, the slug cannot be changed. Choose slugs that are descriptive, lowercase, and hyphenated. Avoid special characters.

**Title** (Required)
The job title as it will appear on the platform (e.g. "Wind Turbine Technician", "Offshore Project Manager"). Be specific and use industry-standard terminology. Check with the Skillnet team if you are unsure whether a role title is widely recognised in the offshore wind sector.

**Sector** (Required)
Select the primary sector that best describes this role. Choose one of:
- Operations & Maintenance
- Marine Operations
- Survey & Design
- Health/Safety/Environment
- Electrical
- Policy & Regulation
- Project Management

**Entry Level** (Required)
The typical entry level for someone starting in this role. Options are:
- **Apprentice** — Formal apprenticeship role, typically requiring secondary education
- **Entry** — Early career, usually requiring a relevant qualification or equivalent experience
- **Mid** — 3–8 years of experience, may include supervisory responsibilities
- **Senior** — 8+ years, typically technical experts or team leads
- **Leadership** — Management or director-level roles

**Description** (Required)
A comprehensive narrative describing the role, its responsibilities, and the work environment. Aim for 150–300 words. Cover:
- What the person does day-to-day
- Where they work (e.g. on a platform, in an office, on a vessel)
- Who they work with
- Key challenges or opportunities

Example: "Wind Turbine Technicians maintain and repair offshore wind turbines. Based on installation vessels and platforms, they perform preventative maintenance, troubleshoot electrical and mechanical systems, and respond to equipment failures. Work is physically demanding and conducted at height, often in challenging weather. Strong technical aptitude and safety discipline are essential."

### Optional Fields

**Salary Range** (Optional)
Minimum and maximum annual salary in EUR. Leave blank if salary information is unavailable or highly variable. If provided, ensure the range is realistic and sourced from recent industry data.

**Key Responsibilities** (Optional)
A bulleted list of the main duties and activities for this role. Include 4–8 items. Examples for a Wind Turbine Technician:
- Conduct routine maintenance inspections on turbine components
- Diagnose and repair electrical faults
- Work safely at height in challenging conditions
- Complete detailed maintenance logs and reports
- Collaborate with other technicians and supervisors

**Qualifications** (Optional)
Prerequisites or typical qualifications for someone in this role. List specific certifications, educational levels, or experience. Examples:
- Leaving Certificate (or equivalent) in STEM subjects
- STCW (Standards of Training, Certification and Watchkeeping) certification
- Height safety ticket or equivalent
- Offshore Safety Induction and Emergency Training (OSIET)

**Working Conditions** (Optional)
A description of the physical environment, schedule, and lifestyle aspects of the role. Important for offshore roles where candidates need to understand shift patterns, offshore accommodation, travel requirements, and climate exposure. Example: "Typically works 14 days on, 21 days off rotation on offshore platforms. Lives in shared accommodation during shifts. Exposed to wind, salt spray, and low temperatures. Safety equipment provided."

**Growth Outlook** (Optional)
An industry narrative about the future demand and growth prospects for this role. Tie this to offshore wind expansion in Ireland and internationally. Example: "Offshore wind energy is growing at 15% annually in European markets. Turbine technicians are in high demand, with significant shortages expected over the next decade. Career progression to senior technician or supervisor roles is common with experience."

**Skills** (Optional)
Link relevant technical and soft skills from the skills database. Select only the skills that are core to success in this role. For a Wind Turbine Technician, examples might include:
- Electrical troubleshooting
- Mechanical maintenance
- Height safety
- Safety systems and procedures
- Teamwork and communication

**Pathway Connections** (Optional)
Define career progressions, lateral moves, or specialisations related to this role. Each pathway has:
- **Target Career** — The role someone might progress to
- **Path Type** — "Progression" (advancement), "Lateral" (sideways move), or "Specialisation" (deeper expertise in a sub-domain)
- **Timeframe** — Typical years or experience needed (e.g. "3–5 years")

Example: A Wind Turbine Technician might have a progression pathway to "Senior Wind Turbine Technician" (3–5 years), a lateral move to "Electrical Offshore Technician" (with additional training), or a specialisation in "Blade Maintenance Specialist" (2–3 years).

**Related Courses** (Optional)
Link training courses that prepare someone for this career. Choose courses that are directly relevant to entry requirements or skill development. If a course appears in "Career Relevance", it will be reciprocally linked here.

### Tips for Career Content

- **Be specific to offshore wind.** Avoid generic job descriptions. Reference offshore platforms, marine equipment, and industry standards unique to the sector.
- **Use consistent terminology.** The offshore wind sector has specific terminology (e.g. "WTG" for wind turbine generator). Use standard terms consistently across all career descriptions.
- **Update annually.** Review salary ranges and growth outlooks each year, especially after industry reports or policy changes.
- **Link skills thoughtfully.** Only select skills that are genuinely core to the role. Too many skill links dilute their value for diagnostic matching.
- **Consider regulatory context.** Flag requirements such as STCW, BOSIET, or maritime certifications explicitly in the description and qualifications.

## Courses

A course represents a formal or informal training programme offered by an education provider, university, private training organisation, or industry body. Courses help users identify learning pathways that prepare them for careers in offshore wind.

### Core Fields

**Slug, Title, Description** (Required)
See the Careers section for guidance on slug and title. For description, provide an overview of what the course covers, the target audience, and learning outcomes. Aim for 100–200 words.

**Provider** (Required)
The name of the organisation delivering the course (e.g. "University College Cork", "Offshore Technology Institute", "Skillnet Ireland"). Be consistent with provider names across the platform; standardise abbreviations and spelling.

**Provider Type** (Required)
Select the primary type of provider:
- **University** — Higher education institution (universities)
- **ETB** — Education and Training Board (Irish public providers)
- **Private** — Private training company or academy
- **Industry** — Industry-led training or professional body
- **Skillnet Network** — Skillnet Ireland member or network training
- **Government** — Government or state-funded programme

### Optional Fields

**Delivery Format** (Optional, but highly recommended)
How the course is delivered:
- **In-Person** — Classroom or workshop
- **Online** — Fully virtual, synchronous or asynchronous
- **Blended** — Mix of online and in-person
- **Self-Paced** — Online, no set schedule

This is critical information for users planning around work or offshore schedules.

**Duration** (Optional, but highly recommended)
How long the course takes in plain language: "5 days", "2 weeks", "1 academic year", "40 hours". If duration varies, use a range: "3–6 months depending on pace".

**Location** (Optional)
The venue or city where the course is taught. For online courses, write "Online". For blended or multiple locations, list the primary location.

**NFQ Level** (Optional)
The National Framework of Qualifications level in Ireland (1–10). This standardises course difficulty:
- **Levels 1–3** — School or basic certification
- **Levels 4–6** — Craft, diploma, or higher certificate
- **Levels 7–8** — Degree (bachelor's)
- **Levels 9–10** — Master's or research qualification

If unsure, ask the provider or the Skillnet team.

**Cost** (Optional)
The course fee in EUR. Enter `0` for free courses. If cost varies by participant type (e.g. employed vs unemployed), add context in the "Cost Notes" field.

**Cost Notes** (Optional)
Funding or affordability context, such as "Fully funded by Skillnet Ireland for eligible participants" or "Subsidised rates available for SME employees". Keep this field brief and up-to-date.

**Next Start Date** (Optional)
The date of the next scheduled course intake. Use the date picker. Update this field after each cohort starts to reflect the next upcoming intake. If intake dates are irregular, add a note in the description.

**Accredited** (Optional)
Tick this box if the course leads to a formal certification recognised by Irish or European qualification frameworks (e.g. NFQ, QQI, ECTS credits).

**Certification Awarded** (Optional)
The name of the certificate, diploma, or award given upon completion (e.g. "QQI Certificate Level 6 in Offshore Renewables Operations"). Only populate this if "Accredited" is ticked.

**Entry Requirements** (Optional)
Prerequisites or prior knowledge assumed. Examples:
- "Secondary education or equivalent numeracy/literacy"
- "3 years offshore experience"
- "STCW Basic Safety Training"
- "None (open to all)"

**Skills** (Optional)
Competencies the course develops. Be selective; choose the 3–5 core skills learners will develop. Link these to related careers so the diagnostic tool can recommend this course to users pursuing those roles.

**Career Relevance** (Optional)
Select careers for which this course is particularly relevant. The platform will show this course in the "Recommended Courses" section on those career pages. Be conservative; only select careers where the course directly prepares someone for entry or advancement.

**Tags** (Optional)
Freeform tags for internal organisation and public filtering. Useful examples:
- `STCW-related`
- `BOSIET`
- `Mechanical`
- `Electrical`
- `Blended`
- `Self-paced`
- `Subsidised`

## Events

Events include workshops, webinars, conferences, networking meetings, and training sessions. Events often include registration and capacity management.

### Core Fields

**Slug, Title, Description** (Required)
See Careers section for slug and title guidance. The description should summarise what the event is about, who should attend, what they will learn, and key speakers or agenda items if relevant. Aim for 100–150 words.

**Type** (Required)
The category of event:
- **Workshop** — Hands-on training with practical activities
- **Webinar** — Online presentation with Q&A
- **Conference** — Multi-day or multi-speaker event
- **Networking** — Informal gathering for professional connections
- **Training** — Formal instruction course offered as an event
- **Roadshow** — Mobile or travelling event visiting multiple locations

**Start Date** (Required)
The date and time when the event begins. Use the date and time picker. Ensure the time is in the local timezone (Irish time if the event is in Ireland). Always set the correct timezone; ambiguous times cause confusion for registrants.

**Location Type** (Required)
How the event is conducted:
- **Physical** — In-person, one or more venues
- **Virtual** — Online only
- **Hybrid** — Both in-person and online attendance options

### Optional Fields

**End Date** (Optional)
For multi-day events (conferences, training blocks), set the date and time of the final day. Leave blank for single-day or short events.

**Location** (Optional)
The venue name or city for physical events (e.g. "Dublin Convention Centre", "Cork"). For virtual events, you may include the platform or URL if relevant (e.g. "Zoom"). For hybrid, list the primary physical venue.

**Capacity** (Optional)
The maximum number of registrations allowed. Once capacity is reached, the registration button is disabled on the public site. Leave blank for unlimited attendance. Update this field carefully; use it for venues with fire safety limits or training sessions with instructor ratios. For webinars, you may leave it blank unless there is a technical limit.

**Image** (Optional)
A hero image for the event. Use a landscape format (16:9 or similar). For in-person events, use photos from the venue or previous editions. For virtual events, use a professional graphic or screenshot. Images help the event stand out on listing pages.

### Event Scheduling and Registrations

Events are always tied to one or more registration campaigns. When you create an event and publish it, the registration system is enabled automatically. Users can register via a form on the event page, and their registrations appear in the admin "Registrations" section with:
- Name, email, phone, organisation, role
- Any dietary requirements or additional notes (if the registration form includes these fields)
- GDPR consent status
- Confirmation status (Pending, Confirmed, Cancelled)

As the event date approaches, review pending registrations. Send confirmations or reminders as needed. See the "Registrations & CSV Export" documentation for how to manage and export registrations.

### Tips for Event Content

- **Always set the timezone correctly.** Ambiguous or incorrect times cause registrants to miss events.
- **Update capacity in real-time.** If you increase the venue capacity or move to a larger room, update the capacity field immediately.
- **Include speaker bios in the description** for high-profile events. Users often decide to attend based on who is speaking.
- **Link to external resources.** If there is an agenda, speaker list, or venue map, include a URL in the description or content field.
- **Manage capacity closely before the event.** Create a reminder to confirm registered attendees a few days before, especially for capacity-limited workshops.

## News Articles

News content includes announcements, industry updates, blog posts, and thought leadership pieces. News helps keep platform users informed about sector developments and platform updates.

### Core Fields

**Slug, Title** (Required)
See Careers section for guidance. News titles should be specific and searchable (e.g. "Ireland's Offshore Wind Capacity Reaches 1.5 GW", not "Big Announcements").

**Date** (Required)
The publication date. This is the date shown to readers and used for sorting on the news listing page. Set this to today's date for new articles, or to the date of an announcement you are publishing retrospectively.

**Excerpt** (Required)
A short summary (1–2 sentences, 50–100 words) that appears on the news listing page and in social media previews. Write this as a reader hook; it should entice people to click and read the full article. Example: "Ireland has achieved a major renewable energy milestone this quarter. Offshore wind now accounts for over 15% of grid capacity, up from 10% last year."

**Content** (Required)
The full article text. Use the rich text editor to format headings, bold text, links, and lists. Aim for 400–800 words for most news articles. Structure your content:
1. **Opening paragraph** — Summarise the news in one strong paragraph
2. **Context** — Why this matters; industry background
3. **Details** — Key facts, figures, quotes, or developments
4. **Conclusion or call-to-action** — What happens next or what readers should know

**Category** (Required)
Choose one primary category for the article. Standard categories include:
- "Announcements" — Platform updates, events, or Skillnet news
- "Industry News" — Sector developments, policy changes, market trends
- "Policy" — Regulatory or government updates affecting offshore wind
- "Research" — Academic findings or industry research releases
- "Career Insights" — Guidance on jobs, skills, or sector employment

**Author** (Required)
The name of the person who wrote or published the article. Use a consistent format across all articles (e.g. "Skillnet Offshore Wind Team" for institutional articles, or "Jane Smith, Skillnet Communications").

### Optional Fields

**Image** (Optional)
A header or hero image for the article. Use landscape format (16:9 or similar). For news articles, use photos relevant to the topic (wind farm, industry event, etc.) or generic offshore/renewable energy imagery. Ensure the image is high-quality and licensed for use.

### Tips for News Content

- **Publish consistently.** Aim for at least 2–4 news items per month to keep the platform current.
- **Link to sources.** Use hyperlinks in the content to link to original news sources, industry reports, or related platform content.
- **Use the excerpt strategically.** Many readers will see only the excerpt on the listing page, so make it count.
- **Update old articles rarely.** Instead of editing, consider publishing a new "Update" article if there is significant new information.
- **Tag with keywords.** Even though "Tags" is not a formal field for News, you can mention keywords in the excerpt or category to improve search visibility.

## Research

Research content includes academic papers, industry reports, white papers, and technical publications. Research content serves users seeking deep dives into offshore wind energy topics.

### Core Fields

**Slug, Title** (Required)
See Careers section for guidance. Research titles are often formal, but should be clear and searchable. Example: "Floating Offshore Wind: Economic Viability and Supply Chain Readiness" is better than "Floating WTG Study".

**Author** (Required)
The lead author or organisation responsible for the research. Examples: "Dr. Michael Johnson", "Trinity College Dublin Department of Engineering".

**Organisation** (Required)
The institution, company, or research body that produced the research. Examples: "University College Cork", "BVG Associates", "EirWind Research Initiative". This helps readers understand the research source and credibility.

**Publication Date** (Required)
The date the research was published or released. Use the date picker. This is used for sorting and helps readers assess the relevance and currency of the research.

**Summary** (Required)
A 150–250 word abstract of the research. Summarise the research question, methodology, key findings, and implications. This should give readers enough information to decide if they want to read the full publication. Example:

"This study examines the economic feasibility of floating offshore wind farms off the Irish coast. Using 10 years of met-ocean and financial data, we model capital costs, operational expenses, and revenue scenarios for a 100 MW floating array. Findings indicate that floating offshore wind can achieve cost parity with fixed-bottom installations by 2030 under realistic deployment and supply chain maturation scenarios. The analysis identifies supply chain constraints and suggests policy interventions to accelerate timeline."

**Categories** (Required)
Select one or more research categories. Standard categories include:
- "Technological Development" — Advances in turbines, platforms, materials
- "Economic Analysis" — Cost, funding, market analysis
- "Environmental Impact" — Ecological, marine, or climate effects
- "Supply Chain & Manufacturing" — Logistics, production, workforce
- "Policy & Regulation" — Government frameworks, standards, licensing
- "Skills & Workforce" — Employment, training, capability development
- "Grid Integration" — Electrical systems, grid balancing, demand

Multiple categories are useful if the research spans multiple domains.

### Optional Fields

**Featured** (Optional)
Tick this box to show this research item in the "Featured Research" section on the homepage. Use this sparingly (5–10 items) to highlight the most important or recent research relevant to the platform's audience.

**Image** (Optional)
A cover image for the research. Use the publication's original cover, or choose a relevant sector image. This helps the research stand out on listing pages.

### Tips for Research Content

- **Preserve publication links.** If the research is available online (academic journal, think tank report), include the URL in the summary or as a follow-up link so readers can access the full publication.
- **Categorise thoughtfully.** Use multiple categories if the research genuinely spans domains. Too many categories dilute usefulness.
- **Review quarterly.** Regularly add new research publications so the research section stays current and valuable.
- **Source diverse perspectives.** Include research from academic institutions, industry bodies, and third-party researchers to provide balanced information.
- **Relate to careers and courses.** When relevant, mention in the summary how the research informs specific career pathways or course content.

## General Best Practices Across All Content Types

1. **Use consistent terminology.** Standardise job titles, organisation names, and technical terms across all content.
2. **Link content together.** Use the skills, pathway, and career relevance features to create a cohesive learning environment.
3. **Set slugs carefully.** Slugs are permanent; once content is live, changing the slug breaks URLs. Plan slugs thoughtfully.
4. **Review before publishing.** Always preview content on the public site to check formatting, links, and images.
5. **Add change notes.** When making significant edits, add a change note explaining why. This helps other editors understand the content evolution.
6. **Keep metadata fresh.** Update salary ranges, course dates, event capacities, and publication links regularly so the platform remains a reliable resource.
